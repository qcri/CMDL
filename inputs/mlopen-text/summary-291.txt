Set in Mongolia's capital city, Ulan Bator, the film combines documentary elements with fictional elements<ref name Die Like a Dog, A lauded Mongolian film probes a mongrel's soul | publisher http://www.time.com/time/asia/asia/magazine/1999/990125/mongolia_dog1.html}} in the fragmented, impressionistic and dreamlike story of Baasar, a dog who dies early in the movie &mdash; shot by a hunter employed by the city to reduce its dog population, which has more than one dog for each four humans in its population of 800,000.<ref name State of Dogs | publisher http://movies.yahoo.com/movie/1809408125/details}} According to Mongolian legend, a dog may be reincarnated in its next life as a human, after roaming free for as long as he wants.<ref name Bestselling author discusses work ‘from a dog’s point of view’ | publisher http://sdranchcoastnews.com/cmv_dm_pages/cmv_dm_feature/2.11_cv_feature/2.11_ft5_luncheon.html}} Baasar roams the memory of his life, uninterested in advancing to a human life.<ref name State of Dogs | publisher http://www.yidff.jp/99/cat049/99c058-2-e.html}} The film includes brief interludes with a solar eclipse, a segment in which a young man recites poems directly to the camera, and a depiction of modern Mongolian life with undercurrents of mysticism and myth.
